WHY (drivers -> constraints)
  Pulling demand:
    • Buyers keep placing more accelerator orders than Nvidia and its suppliers can build, so lead times and rental prices stay high.  (NVDA demand durability)  (4 sources)
    • Providers are raising on-demand GPU rental prices as compute stays scarce, so rates hold or climb even as more capacity comes online.  (Pricing power persistence)  (4 sources)
  Capping supply:
    • Memory makers move capacity to HBM and hold back DRAM, so the memory and packaging bottleneck caps how many accelerators actually ship.  (Supply constraint binding)  (3 sources)
  Contested:
    • Buyers seeking a non-Nvidia option are turning to custom ASICs rather than AMD, so AMD's roadmap is not translating into datacenter share.  (AMD credible second source — medium conviction)  (2 sources)
    • Aggressive enforcement against unlicensed China shipments keeps the legal, licensed channel narrow, so any revenue recovery there stays small.  (Conditional China re-opening with a capped ceiling — low conviction)  (1 source)
    • A frontier model was trained entirely on non-Nvidia ASICs, showing teams can run large AI workloads without Nvidia's software stack when they must.  (CUDA software lock-in sustains the moat — early — not yet corroborated)  (2 sources)
    • Hyperscalers and Chinese firms build their own or domestic accelerators for some workloads, but this adds to total AI silicon demand rather than cutting Nvidia GPU orders.  (Custom ASIC substitution — CHALLENGED ⚠)  (2 sources)
    • Customers keep buying complete Grace Blackwell systems that come with Nvidia networking, so the bundle raises the cost of switching to standalone accelerators.  (Networking attach as systems moat — low conviction)  (1 source)
    • Memory makers are moving capacity to high-margin HBM and holding back standard DRAM, so the memory portion of each AI-server bill of materials keeps rising.  (Rising memory costs inflate AI-server economics — early — not yet corroborated)  (sources in history)
