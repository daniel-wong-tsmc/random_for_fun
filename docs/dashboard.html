Where the market stands

Strong · Worsening
Main limiting factor: Supply bottleneck

Demand keeps growing and staying strong, backed by six-year GPU rental cloud deals and vendor financial guarantees covering well over 200,000 GPUs. But the main bottleneck has shifted from enforcement of US limits on chip sales to China, to memory: three publishers report that manufacturers are shifting production away from standard memory chips toward high-bandwidth memory, and fast storage drives are also scarce. That is pushing rack costs up 2 to 3 percent every week and now limits how many systems can actually ship.

Vendor-financed demand circularity 🆕 New🏛 Official source💲 High impact
Still holds · Getting stronger · Confidence: high · Tracked for 2 runs · 4 sources
More and more of the demand for Nvidia's AI chips is being funded by Nvidia's own revenue-sharing and financing deals. That means the demand strength Nvidia reports is increasingly tied to credit Nvidia itself is extending, adding "circular financing" risk to how durable this buildout really is.
We'd change our mind if …
NVIDIA's quarterly SEC filing shows customer financing or notes receivable rising for 2 consecutive quarters, or a named financed GPU rental cloud customer fails to convert its financed commitment to cash revenue within 1 quarter.

Networking attach as systems moat 🏛 Official source
Still holds · Reconfirmed · Confidence: low · Tracked for 4 runs · 1 sources
Nvidia now sells more data-center networking switches — the hardware that connects servers together — than any other company, and bundles that networking with its GPUs. This deepens its full-package advantage and makes it harder for customers to switch to standalone AI chips or custom-built silicon. In the first quarter of 2026, Nvidia took the top revenue spot in data-center networking switches by pairing its Spectrum-X and Quantum networking products with its Blackwell and GB300 chips, and its NVLink connection technology lowers the cost of running AI models per unit of output. However, no evidence found this run actually compares how fast networking sales are growing versus how fast chip sales are growing, so the stronger claim that networking is growing faster than computing is not backed up.
We'd change our mind if …
NVIDIA networking revenue grows slower than Data Center compute revenue for 2 consecutive quarters, or networking falls below 15% of Data Center revenue per Market share.
